<p>(6th Part of the 7-Part series on what it takes to be a strong ML engineer in 2026)</p><p><strong>The hardest lesson I’m learning about ML engineering:</strong></p><p>Technical excellence is not enough.</p><p>You can build the perfect model. Implement flawless pipelines. Have bulletproof monitoring. Deploy with zero downtime.</p><p>And still completely fail to deliver value.</p><p>Why? Two reasons that nobody talks about enough:</p><ol><li><strong>You didn’t measure the right things (evals)</strong></li><li><strong>The organization wasn’t set up for success (organizational context)</strong></li></ol><p>Let me share what I’m learning about both.</p><h3>Evals: The Difference Between a Demo and a Product</h3><p>Here’s the uncomfortable pattern I keep seeing:</p><p><strong>Month 1:</strong> “Look at this amazing POC! 95% accuracy!” <br><strong>Month 3: </strong>“We’re still working on production readiness…” <br><strong>Month 6:</strong> “We decided not to move forward with this.”</p><p>What happened?</p><p><strong>They didn’t have real evals.</strong></p><p>They had metrics that looked good in development. But those metrics didn’t predict production success. Didn’t correlate with user value. Didn’t catch the failure modes that mattered.</p><p><strong>If you don’t have rigorous evaluation, your POC has a ~90% chance of never reaching production.</strong></p><p>Not because the model doesn’t work. Because you can’t prove it works, can’t measure when it degrades, and can’t iterate systematically.</p><p><strong>What I’m learning about real evaluation:</strong></p><p><strong>Offline evals that actually predict online performance:</strong></p><p><strong>This is harder than it sounds. Most offline evals lie.</strong></p><p><strong>What makes offline evals predictive:</strong></p><h3>Test sets that represent production distribution:</h3><ul><li>Not just “held-out data from the same source”</li><li>But data that looks like what you’ll see in production</li><li>Including edge cases, adversarial examples, distribution shifts</li><li>Updated regularly as production distribution changes</li></ul><h3>Metrics that correlate with business value:</h3><ul><li>Not just “accuracy” or “F1 score”</li><li>But metrics that predict user satisfaction, retention, revenue</li><li>Sometimes a “worse” model on traditional metrics wins on business metrics</li><li>Measure what actually matters, not what’s easy to measure</li></ul><h3>Subgroup analysis:</h3><ul><li>Overall accuracy: 90%</li><li>Accuracy on critical subgroup: 60%</li><li>You just found a disaster waiting to happen</li><li>Always slice your metrics by important dimensions</li></ul><h3>Adversarial examples:</h3><ul><li>Inputs designed to break your model</li><li>Edge cases that seem unlikely but will happen at scale</li><li>Systematic testing of failure modes</li><li>If you don’t test it, production will</li></ul><p><strong>Lesson: Your test set needs to be adversarial, not just representative.</strong></p><h3>Online evals that measure reality:</h3><p>Offline metrics tell you what might happen. Online metrics tell you what actually happened.</p><p><strong>What I’m learning to measure online:</strong></p><h3>A/B testing with statistical rigor:</h3><ul><li>Not just “model A vs. model B”</li><li>But proper sample sizes, statistical significance, confidence intervals</li><li>Account for novelty effects (early results often don’t hold)</li><li>Run long enough to see long-term impacts</li></ul><h3>Metrics that matter:</h3><ul><li>Click-through rate</li><li>Time spent</li><li>Conversion rate</li><li>User retention</li><li>Not just “model accuracy”</li></ul><h3>Leading indicators of problems:</h3><ul><li>Metrics that degrade before users complain</li><li>Changes in prediction distribution</li><li>Shifts in feature values</li><li>Increases in model uncertainty</li><li>Catch problems early, before they compound</li></ul><p><strong>Real-world example:</strong></p><ul><li>Recommendation system A/B test</li><li>Model B had slightly better offline metrics</li><li>Model B launched to 5% of users</li><li>Engagement went up initially (novelty effect)</li><li>But retention dropped after a week (users got bored of narrow recommendations)</li><li>Good thing: canary deployment caught this before full rollout</li><li>Offline evals missed the long-term retention impact entirely</li></ul><h3>Continuous monitoring as ongoing evaluation:</h3><p>Evaluation doesn’t stop after deployment. It’s continuous.</p><p><strong>What to monitor:</strong></p><h3>Model performance over time:</h3><ul><li>Are metrics degrading?</li><li>On which subgroups?</li><li>How fast?</li><li>What’s causing it?</li></ul><h3>Distribution drift detection:</h3><ul><li>Input distributions changing</li><li>Output distributions changing</li><li>Feature correlations changing</li><li>Triggers for retraining</li></ul><h3>User feedback loops:</h3><ul><li>Explicit feedback (ratings, reports)</li><li>Implicit feedback (engagement, behavior)</li><li>Qualitative feedback (support tickets, surveys)</li><li>Close the loop from feedback to model improvement</li></ul><h3>Eval pipelines that run automatically:</h3><ul><li>On every model change</li><li>On every data change</li><li>On schedule (daily, weekly)</li><li>Before any deployment</li></ul><h3>Regression test suites:</h3><ul><li>“Model must maintain &gt;X% on critical subgroup Y”</li><li>“Model must not predict Z when feature W is present”</li><li>“Model latency must stay under N milliseconds at p99”</li><li>Fail the deployment if any regression test fails</li></ul><h3>Eval dashboards:</h3><ul><li>Real-time view of online metrics</li><li>Historical trends</li><li>Breakdowns by subgroup, geography, time</li><li>Alerts when metrics degrade</li></ul><blockquote><em>If you can’t measure it, you can’t improve it. And you probably shouldn’t deploy it.</em></blockquote><h3>Optimization: Making Models Economically Viable</h3><p>This is the part that’s humbling me right now.</p><p>A model that costs $10 per inference will never scale to production. No matter how good it is.</p><p><strong>Real constraints I’m learning about:</strong></p><h3>Cost per inference:</h3><ul><li>What does each prediction actually cost?</li><li>Token costs (for LLMs)</li><li>Compute costs (GPU time)</li><li>Infrastructure costs (serving, storage)</li><li>Can you afford this at scale?</li></ul><h3>Latency requirements:</h3><ul><li>Users won’t wait 10 seconds</li><li>Your model needs to be fast enough for your use case</li><li>Or it doesn’t matter how accurate it is</li></ul><h3>Throughput needs:</h3><ul><li>How many requests per second?</li><li>Can your infrastructure handle peak load?</li><li>What’s the cost at peak vs. average?</li></ul><p><strong>What I’m learning about optimization:</strong></p><h3>Model compression techniques:</h3><h3>Distillation:</h3><ul><li>Train small model to mimic large model</li><li>Can achieve 90%+ of performance at 10% of size</li><li>When to use: need to preserve quality, willing to invest in training</li><li>Trade compute during training for efficiency during serving</li></ul><h3>Quantization:</h3><ul><li>Reduce precision of weights and activations</li><li>FP32 → FP16 or INT8 or even INT4</li><li>Dramatic speedups and memory savings</li><li>Some accuracy loss (how much depends on the model and task)</li><li>When to use: when you can afford small accuracy drops for big efficiency gains</li></ul><h3>Pruning:</h3><ul><li>Remove unnecessary parameters</li><li>Surprisingly many weights can be zeroed out</li><li>Reduces model size and compute</li><li>Requires careful validation to avoid breaking the model</li></ul><h3>Caching strategies:</h3><h3>Prompt caching:</h3><ul><li>Cache embeddings for repeated prompts</li><li>Huge savings for common queries</li><li>Works great when queries are repetitive</li><li>Doesn’t help much for unique queries</li></ul><h3>Result caching:</h3><ul><li>Cache outputs for repeated inputs</li><li>Instant responses for cache hits</li><li>But: cache invalidation is hard</li><li>But: how do you handle slight variations in input?</li></ul><h3>KV cache optimization:</h3><ul><li>Reuse cached key-value pairs in attention</li><li>Critical for LLM serving efficiency</li><li>Manage cache size vs. context length tradeoffs</li></ul><h3>Prompt compression:</h3><ul><li>Shorter prompts = fewer tokens = lower cost</li><li>Remove unnecessary words</li><li>Use abbreviations where possible</li><li>But: don’t compress so much you lose meaning</li></ul><h3><strong>What actually works (from people running this in production):</strong></h3><p><strong>“We distilled GPT-4 to a smaller model for our specific use case. 95% of the quality at 5% of the cost.”</strong></p><p><strong>“Quantization to INT8 gave us 3x speedup with negligible quality loss for our task.”</strong></p><p><strong>“Caching saved us hundreds of thousands in LLM costs. 40% of our queries were effectively duplicates.”</strong></p><p><strong>“We optimized prompts from 1000 tokens to 300 tokens. Same quality, 70% cost reduction.”</strong></p><p>Every optimization is a tradeoff:</p><ul><li>Quality vs. speed</li><li>Quality vs. cost</li><li>Latency vs. throughput</li><li>Accuracy vs. efficiency</li></ul><h3>Organizational Context: Even Perfect Engineering Fails in Broken Systems</h3><p>Here’s the part that most technical posts completely ignore.</p><p>And it’s the part that I’m realizing might matter most.</p><p><strong>You can be an exceptional ML engineer and still completely fail if the organization isn’t set up for success.</strong></p><p><strong>Real failure modes I’m learning about:</strong></p><h3>Data pipelines owned by teams that don’t understand ML:</h3><p><strong>What happens:</strong></p><ul><li>ML team needs features with specific properties</li><li>Data team builds pipeline that seems reasonable but breaks ML assumptions</li><li>ML team discovers problems months later</li><li>Finger-pointing ensues</li><li>Nobody wins</li></ul><p><strong>What’s needed:</strong></p><ul><li>Shared understanding of ML requirements</li><li>Clear contracts and SLAs</li><li>Regular communication</li><li>Mutual respect for each other’s expertise</li></ul><h3>Deployment processes that make iteration impossible:</h3><p><strong>What happens:</strong></p><ul><li>Model needs emergency update (data drift, critical bug)</li><li>Deployment requires 3 weeks of approvals</li><li>By the time it’s approved, the problem has compounded</li><li>Users have bad experience, trust erodes</li></ul><p><strong>What’s needed:</strong></p><ul><li>Streamlined deployment for pre-approved model updates</li><li>Emergency procedures for critical issues</li><li>Trust in ML team’s judgment (earned through reliability)</li><li>Fast rollback capabilities</li></ul><h3>Monitoring systems that can’t handle ML metrics:</h3><p><strong>What happens:</strong></p><ul><li>ML team needs to monitor distribution drift, calibration, subgroup performance</li><li>Existing monitoring only supports basic metrics (latency, errors, throughput)</li><li>ML-specific problems go undetected</li><li>Models degrade silently</li></ul><p><strong>What’s needed:</strong></p><ul><li>Monitoring infrastructure that supports custom metrics</li><li>Investment in ML-specific observability</li><li>Buy-in from infrastructure teams</li></ul><h3>Success metrics that don’t reflect value:</h3><p><strong>What happens:</strong></p><ul><li>ML team optimizes for accuracy</li><li>Business cares about revenue</li><li>Model gets 95% accuracy, revenue stays flat</li><li>Business: “What was the point?”</li><li>ML team: “But the model is great!”</li></ul><p><strong>What’s needed:</strong></p><ul><li>Alignment on what success actually means</li><li>Metrics that connect model performance to business outcomes</li><li>Regular check-ins to ensure alignment</li></ul><p><strong>What You Can Do:</strong></p><h3>Build relationships with data engineering:</h3><ul><li>Understand their constraints and challenges</li><li>Explain ML requirements clearly</li><li>Collaborate on pipeline design</li><li>Create shared ownership of data quality</li></ul><h3>Advocate for ML-friendly infrastructure:</h3><ul><li>Make the case for necessary tooling</li><li>Show ROI of proper monitoring, deployment, etc.</li><li>Start small, demonstrate value, expand</li><li>Don’t ask for everything at once</li></ul><h3>Educate stakeholders on meaningful metrics:</h3><ul><li>Explain why accuracy isn’t enough</li><li>Connect model metrics to business outcomes</li><li>Set realistic expectations</li><li>Celebrate real wins, not vanity metrics</li></ul><h3>Create feedback loops:</h3><ul><li>From model performance to business outcomes</li><li>From user feedback to model improvements</li><li>From production issues to training data</li><li>From online metrics to offline evaluation</li></ul><p><strong>ML engineering is not just technical. It’s organizational.</strong></p><p>You’re not just building models. You’re building systems that exist in organizations with people, processes, politics, and constraints.</p><p>Ignoring the organizational context doesn’t make it go away. It just means you’ll be surprised when it blocks you.</p><p><strong>Real stories that illustrate this:</strong></p><p><strong>Story 1: Perfect model, broken deployment</strong></p><ul><li>Team built amazing model</li><li>Deployment process required 6 weeks of security reviews</li><li>By the time it was approved, the business priorities had changed</li><li>Model never deployed</li><li>Lesson: Understand deployment constraints early</li></ul><p><strong>Story 2: Model that measured the wrong thing</strong></p><ul><li>Team optimized for click-through rate</li><li>Business wanted revenue</li><li>CTR went up, revenue went down (people clicked but didn’t buy)</li><li>Misaligned incentives from the start</li><li>Lesson: Align on success metrics before building</li></ul><p><strong>Story 3: Data pipeline that broke ML assumptions</strong></p><ul><li>Data team built aggregations that seemed reasonable</li><li>But introduced temporal leakage for ML use case</li><li>ML team discovered this 3 months into training</li><li>Had to rebuild from scratch</li><li>Lesson: Involve ML in data pipeline design</li></ul><p>The best ML engineers I’m learning from aren’t just technically excellent.</p><p>They’re also:</p><ul><li>Great at measurement and evaluation</li><li>Conscious about economic constraints</li><li>Skilled at navigating organizations</li><li>Effective at communication and alignment</li></ul><p><strong>Technical skills are necessary. But not sufficient.</strong></p><p><strong>What organizational challenges have blocked your ML work? What would you do differently?</strong></p><p>And as always, if you have experience with evals, optimization, or organizational ML that I’m missing please share it. This is how we all learn.</p><h3>Tomorrow: Part 7 (Final)</h3><p>Putting it all together. What this actually means for becoming a great ML engineer in 2026.</p><p>Building real systems end-to-end. Learning from failure. The path forward.</p>
