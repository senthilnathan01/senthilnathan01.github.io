<p>(4th Part of the 7-Part series on what it takes to be a strong ML engineer in 2026)</p><p><strong>The moment I realized I didn’t understand production ML:</strong></p><p>I had built a model. It worked beautifully on my laptop. 95% accuracy on my test set. Clean code. Fast inference.</p><p>Then I tried to deploy it.</p><p>Suddenly I needed to think about: data pipelines that don’t break, features that are actually available at inference time, monitoring that catches problems before users do, deployments that don’t take down the service, and a dozen other things that had nothing to do with model architecture.</p><p>That’s when it hit me: <strong>Building the model is maybe 20% of the work. The other 80% is everything around it.</strong></p><p>Let me share what I’m learning about that other 80%.</p><h3>Data Pipelines: Where ML engineering actually happens</h3><p>Here’s something nobody told me when I was learning ML from tutorials:</p><p><strong>Your model is only as good as the data flowing into it.</strong></p><p>Not the training data you carefully curated. The messy production data that arrives late, incomplete, or broken.</p><h3>Feature stores are Harder than they look</h3><p>The promise: Store features once, use them for training and serving.</p><p>The reality:</p><ul><li>Online-offline consistency is brutally hard to maintain</li><li>Training uses batch features computed with all the time in the world</li><li>Serving needs low-latency features computed in real-time</li><li>These are fundamentally different systems</li><li>Keeping them consistent? That’s an entire challenge</li></ul><h3>Point-in-time correctness:</h3><ul><li>Training needs to see the world as it looked at prediction time</li><li>Not as it looks now with all future information</li><li>This is harder than it sounds</li><li>Get it wrong = temporal leakage = useless model</li></ul><p><strong>Real example I studied:</strong></p><ul><li>Model predicting customer churn</li><li>Training used “customer’s total spend” as a feature</li><li>But that feature included spend AFTER the prediction date</li><li>Model learned: “customers who spend a lot don’t churn” (obviously)</li><li>In production: feature only includes past spend</li><li>Model performance: completely different</li></ul><p><strong>Feature freshness:</strong></p><ul><li>Some features update every second</li><li>Some update daily</li><li>Some update never</li><li>Your model expects fresh data</li><li>What happens when the upstream pipeline breaks?</li></ul><h3>What I’m building into my mental model:</h3><p><strong>Late-arriving data:</strong></p><ul><li>Events arrive out of order</li><li>Data processing has delays</li><li>What you see at prediction time ≠ what you see an hour later</li><li>How do you handle this?</li></ul><p><strong>Schema evolution:</strong></p><ul><li>Upstream teams change their data schemas</li><li>Your features depend on fields that disappear</li><li>Or get renamed</li><li>Or change types</li><li>Without telling you</li></ul><p><strong>Backfills:</strong></p><ul><li>Need to retrain with new features</li><li>Must recompute features historically</li><li>Without creating temporal leakage</li><li>While keeping the production system running</li><li>This is where most backfills fail</li></ul><p>Your model degraded? Probably upstream data changed. Your training failed? Probably a schema migration broke your feature join. Your predictions are wrong? Probably you’re reading stale features.</p><p><strong>Fix the pipeline first. Then worry about the model.</strong></p><h3>Monitoring: If you’re not measuring it, it’s failing silently</h3><p>Traditional software monitoring: Is the service up? What’s the latency? Any errors?</p><p>ML monitoring: All of that, plus a dozen model-specific things that can degrade silently while your service runs “normally.”</p><p><strong>What I’m learning to monitor:</strong></p><p><strong>Model-specific metrics:</strong></p><h3>Prediction distribution drift:</h3><ul><li>Your model starts predicting class A way more often</li><li>Or the distribution of predicted probabilities shifts</li><li>This might be fine (world changed)</li><li>Or catastrophic (model broke)</li><li>You need to know which</li></ul><h3>Feature distribution drift:</h3><ul><li>Input features start looking different</li><li>Means change, variances change</li><li>New values appear that weren’t in training</li><li>This breaks models in subtle ways</li></ul><h3>Calibration degradation:</h3><ul><li>Model says “90% confident”</li><li>Actually right 60% of the time</li><li>Your decision thresholds are now completely wrong</li></ul><p><strong>Subgroup performance disparities:</strong></p><ul><li>Overall accuracy: 90% (great!)</li><li>Accuracy on demographic group X: 60% (disaster)</li><li>Without explicit subgroup monitoring, you’ll never catch this</li></ul><p><strong>For LLM systems specifically:</strong></p><h3>Hallucination rates:</h3><ul><li>What percentage of outputs contain ungrounded claims?</li><li>Which types of queries trigger hallucinations?</li><li>Is this rate increasing over time?</li></ul><h3>Token consumption:</h3><ul><li>Cost per request</li><li>Token usage trends</li><li>Unexpected spikes (someone’s abusing your system?)</li></ul><h3>Retrieval quality (for RAG):</h3><ul><li>Are you retrieving relevant documents?</li><li>Hit rates on your vector database</li><li>Latency of retrieval operations</li><li>Quality of retrieved chunks</li></ul><p><strong>Operational metrics that matter:</strong></p><h3>Per-layer latency breakdowns:</h3><ul><li>Where is your model actually slow?</li><li>Is it tokenization? Embedding? Generation?</li><li>Can’t optimize what you don’t measure</li></ul><h3>GPU utilization:</h3><ul><li>Are you actually using your expensive GPUs?</li><li>Waiting on data loading?</li><li>Memory-bound or compute-bound?</li></ul><h3>Queue depths and backpressure:</h3><ul><li>How many requests are waiting?</li><li>Are you falling behind?</li><li>When do you start rejecting requests?</li></ul><h3>The hard part: Turning metrics into actionable alerts</h3><p>Bad alert: “Model accuracy dropped 2%”</p><ul><li>Could be noise</li><li>Could be normal variation</li><li>Probably gets ignored</li></ul><p>Good alert: “Model accuracy dropped 15% on users from Region X in the last 6 hours, 99.9% confidence this is real”</p><ul><li>Specific</li><li>Significant</li><li>Localized</li><li>Actionable</li></ul><h3>Deployment: Not just pushing to production. Managing risk.</h3><p>I used to think deployment was: git push, docker build, kubectl apply, done.</p><p>Then I learned about all the ways that approach causes production incidents.</p><p><strong>What I’m learning about safe deployment:</strong></p><h3>Model versioning that’s actually traceable:</h3><ul><li>Not just “model_v2.pkl”</li><li>Which training data?</li><li>Which code version?</li><li>Which hyperparameters?</li><li>Which person approved it?</li><li>Full lineage from data to deployed model</li></ul><h3>Shadow deployments:</h3><ul><li>New model runs alongside old model</li><li>New model sees production traffic</li><li>But doesn’t affect user experience</li><li>Compare outputs, latency, error rates</li><li>Catch problems before they impact users</li></ul><h3>Canary releases:</h3><ul><li>Deploy to 1% of traffic</li><li>Monitor carefully</li><li>Gradual rollout: 1% → 5% → 25% → 100%</li><li>Automatic rollback if metrics degrade</li></ul><h3>A/B testing frameworks:</h3><ul><li>Not just “which model is better”</li><li>But accounting for:</li></ul><p><strong>Rollback procedures that work at 2am:</strong></p><ul><li>Not “submit a ticket and wait for approval”</li><li>Not “manually revert the deployment”</li><li>Automated: detect degradation → trigger rollback → notify on-call</li><li>Practice this. Regularly.</li><li>When production is on fire, muscle memory matters</li></ul><p><strong>Real story I heard:</strong></p><ul><li>Team deployed new model Friday afternoon</li><li>Metrics looked fine initially</li><li>By Monday, customer complaints flooding in</li><li>Took 4 hours to rollback (manual process)</li><li>Lost customers, lost trust</li><li>Now they have one-click rollback tested weekly</li></ul><h3>Distributed Systems: ML is just distributed systems with gradients</h3><p>Your ML system doesn’t exist in isolation. It’s part of a distributed system with all the classic distributed systems problems.</p><h3>Retry logic with exponential backoff:</h3><ul><li>Request failed? Retry.</li><li>Failed again? Wait longer, retry.</li><li>Keep failing? Eventually give up.</li><li>But: don’t retry on errors that will always fail</li><li>And: be careful not to create retry storms</li></ul><h3>Idempotency:</h3><ul><li>Request gets processed twice (network glitch, retry, whatever)</li><li>Does this create duplicate predictions?</li><li>Duplicate charges?</li><li>Duplicate database entries?</li><li>Make your endpoints idempotent or suffer</li></ul><h3>Partial failures:</h3><ul><li>Feature store is down</li><li>Fall back to cached features? Default values? Fail the request?</li><li>Retrieval service is slow</li><li>Wait? Timeout? Use stale cache?</li><li>Model service crashed</li><li>Route to backup? Queue and retry? Error immediately?</li></ul><h3>Rate limiting and circuit breakers:</h3><ul><li>Downstream service is struggling</li><li>Don’t make it worse by hammering it with requests</li><li>Back off. Give it time to recover.</li><li>Circuit breaker: stop requests after N failures, wait, try again</li></ul><p>These aren’t hypothetical problems. They happen in production. Regularly.</p><p>Your model might be perfect. But if your distributed systems fundamentals are weak, users will have a bad time anyway.</p><h3>What I’ve heard from other engineers:</h3><p><strong>“We spent 3 months optimizing model accuracy. It failed in production because our feature pipeline had a 5-minute delay we didn’t account for.”</strong></p><p><strong>“Our model was great. Our deployment process was terrible. We took down production three times before we built proper canary releases.”</strong></p><p><strong>“We monitored overall metrics. Didn’t notice we were performing terribly on 10% of users until they complained.”</strong></p><p><strong>“Shadow mode saved us. New model looked good in offline eval. In shadow mode, we caught that it was 3x slower than the old model under real load.”</strong></p><p><strong>“We didn’t have automatic rollback. When the model failed at 2am, it took us 6 hours to manually revert. Never again.”</strong></p><p>The model is the easy part (relatively). The system around it: the pipelines, monitoring, deployment, failure handling, that’s where so much more engineering happens.</p><p>And that’s okay. That’s the job.</p><h3>Next: Part 5</h3><p>The critical pieces everyone forgets: Observability that actually helps debug, agents and their failure modes, security (prompt injection is real), and why your documentation matters more than you think</p>
