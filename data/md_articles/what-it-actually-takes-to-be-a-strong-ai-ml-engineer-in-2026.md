<p><strong>I have been analysing what it takes to be a strong AI/ML engineer in 2026</strong></p><p>So I’m starting a <strong>7-part series</strong> this week to break down the fundamentals that actually matter.</p><p>This is <strong>not a roadmap</strong> and definitely not an attempt to be exhaustive. Instead, I’m treating this as a <strong>working checklist </strong>of primary concepts that feel non-negotiable to me right now.</p><p>The reality is: AI is vast. Even within a single subdomain like computer vision, NLP the checklist can explode endlessly. Trying to cover everything is a trap.</p><p>So this is a <strong>small, opinionated checklist</strong>. Incomplete. Evolving. But better than staring at infinite possibilities and doing nothing.</p><p>I don’t have deep industry experience yet, so I’m sure I’ll miss things. I’d rather be corrected and learn than be polite and stay wrong.</p><p><strong>If you’re also on this journey — learning ML engineering, trying to move from tutorials to production systems, figuring out what actually matters — I hope this series helps</strong>.</p><p>And if you’re already where I’m trying to get to, I hope you’ll share what I’m missing.</p><p>Each post will cover a different aspect of what I’m learning:</p><ul><li>Part 1: The Foundation — Data, Statistics, and the basics everyone skips</li><li>Part 2: Training and Loss Functions (What you’re actually optimizing)</li><li>Part 3: LLMs and Modern ML (The new fundamentals)</li><li>Part 4: Production Systems (Where models die)</li><li>Part 5: The Critical Pieces (Observability, Agents, Security)</li><li>Part 6: Evals and Organizational Reality (What actually determines success)</li><li>Part 7: Putting It All Together (What This Actually Means)</li></ul><h3>Part 1: The Foundation (Stop Fooling Yourself)</h3><p>The uncomfortable truth about AI/ML engineering in 2026:</p><p>I keep seeing the same pattern as I dive deeper into this field: Engineers who can recite transformer architecture in their sleep but can’t explain why their model failed in production. Data scientists who optimize for benchmark metrics that have zero correlation with business value. Teams celebrating 99% accuracy on datasets that don’t represent reality.</p><p>So let’s get honest about what actually matters by starting with the foundations most people skip.</p><h3>1. Start with the data, or fail with the model.</h3><p>Before you touch a single line of PyTorch, before you even think about model architecture, you need to understand your data.</p><p>Not “I ran df.describe()” understand. I mean:</p><h3>Where does this data come from, and what incentives shaped its collection?</h3><ul><li>Was it collected by humans who get paid per label? (Hello, label noise)</li><li>Is it from a system that only logs successful transactions? (Survivorship bias)</li><li>Does it come from users who opted in? (Selection bias)</li></ul><h3>What systematic biases exist in the labeling process?</h3><ul><li>Who labeled this data, and what assumptions did they bring?</li><li>Are some classes easier to label than others?</li><li>Do label quality vary by annotator, time of day, or fatigue level?</li></ul><h3>How will this distribution shift in production?</h3><ul><li>Training on daytime data, deploying for 24/7 usage</li><li>Building on data from one demographic, serving everyone</li><li>Learning from historical patterns that are actively changing</li></ul><p><strong>What are the data generation mechanisms you’re assuming are stable?</strong> Because they’re probably not.</p><p>Here’s what nobody tells you when you’re starting: <strong>80% of ML failures aren’t model failures. They’re data failures that models faithfully learned.</strong></p><p>That label noise you decided to ignore? Your model memorized it. That sampling bias in your training set? Now it’s systematic discrimination in production. That temporal leak you missed during train/test split? Congratulations, your “95% accurate” model is completely useless.</p><h3>2. Statistics isn’t optional anymore. It’s survival.</h3><p>We can code. We can build neural networks. But can we actually reason about uncertainty? About what our metrics mean? About when we’re fooling ourselves?</p><p><strong>You need to understand:</strong></p><p><strong>Bias vs. Variance </strong>— Not just the definitions, but the actual tradeoffs:</p><ul><li>High bias = your model is too simple to capture the pattern</li><li>High variance = your model memorized the training set</li><li>The sweet spot is somewhere in between, and it’s <strong>different for every problem.</strong></li></ul><p><strong>Confidence Intervals </strong>— Because point estimates without uncertainty are dangerous:</p><ul><li>Your model’s accuracy is 87%… ± what?</li><li>Is that difference between models statistically significant or just noise?</li><li>How confident should you actually be in this prediction?</li></ul><p><strong>Calibration</strong> — Your model’s confidence scores are probably lying:</p><ul><li>A model that says “90% confident” should be right 90% of the time</li><li>Most models are overconfident on mistakes (the worst possible combination)</li><li>Calibration matters more than accuracy for decision-making systems</li></ul><p><strong>Distribution Shift </strong>— The silent killer of production models:</p><ul><li>Your training distribution is not your production distribution</li><li>Covariate shift: inputs change, relationship stays the same</li><li>Concept drift: the underlying relationship changes</li><li>Label shift: the frequency of classes changes</li></ul><p><strong>Why “95% accuracy” is often meaningless:</strong></p><ul><li>Accuracy on what baseline? (Predicting “no fraud” gets you 99% accuracy if fraud is 1%)</li><li>On which subgroups? (Great overall, terrible on minorities)</li><li>Under what distribution? (Perfect on your test set, useless in production)</li></ul><p>The difference between a good ML engineer and a great one often comes down to statistical literacy.</p><p>Someone who sees “95% accuracy” and asks: “Accuracy on what? Compared to what baseline? On which subgroups? Under what distribution? What’s the confidence interval?”</p><h3>3. One stack, deeply. Not five stacks, shallowly.</h3><p>Here’s my current take (and I’m open to being wrong): <strong>Pick PyTorch or JAX. Then go deep.</strong></p><p>I don’t mean reading the documentation. I mean:</p><h3>Understanding GPU memory hierarchies:</h3><ul><li>Why your batch size matters beyond “bigger is better”</li><li>What actually happens when you run out of VRAM</li><li>How gradient checkpointing trades compute for memory</li><li>Where your memory is actually going</li></ul><h3>Mixed precision training:</h3><ul><li>What FP16, BF16, and FP32 actually mean</li><li>Where numerical instability creeps in with lower precision</li><li>How automatic mixed precision decides what to keep in FP32</li><li>Why some operations need higher precision and others don’t</li></ul><h3>Profiling and optimization:</h3><ul><li>How to actually identify your training bottleneck</li><li>Is it data loading? Computation? GPU-CPU transfer?</li><li>Which operations are surprisingly slow and why</li><li>Kernel fusion and why some operations should be combined</li></ul><h3>Why your model OOMs at 3am:</h3><ul><li>It’s usually not the model weights</li><li>It’s the optimizer states (2–3x model size for Adam)</li><li>It’s the activations you’re storing for backprop</li><li>It’s the intermediate tensors you forgot about</li></ul><p>When your model OOMs during a critical training run, “just reduce batch size” isn’t engineering.</p><p>Knowing exactly which layer is holding what tensors, why the optimizer needs that memory, and which tradeoffs you can make. That’s engineering!</p><h3>What I’m learning (the hard way)</h3><p>As I dig deeper into this, a few things are becoming clear:</p><p>1.<strong> The fundamentals are not sexy, but they’re essential.</strong> Nobody gets excited about understanding data pipelines or statistical testing. But that’s where most problems hide.</p><p>2. <strong>You can’t skip steps. </strong>I tried. I wanted to jump straight to building transformer models. But without understanding data quality, loss functions, and evaluation properly, I was just guessing with expensive compute.</p><p>3. <strong>Depth beats breadth at this stage.</strong> I’m focusing on PyTorch. Really learning it. Not adding TensorFlow and JAX and MLX to my resume. One stack, deeply understood, is more valuable than surface knowledge of five.</p><p>4. <strong>Production is a different world.</strong> The gap between “it works on my laptop” and “it works reliably in production serving real users” is massive. Most learning resources ignore this gap entirely.</p><h3>Tomorrow: Part 2</h3><p>Training and loss functions — what you’re actually optimizing, and why most loss functions silently create bad products.</p><p><strong>If you’re also learning this stuff, follow along. If you’re ahead of me, please share what I’m missing.</strong></p><p>Every comment helps me (and everyone else reading this) learn.</p><p>What foundation do you wish you’d built stronger before going deeper into ML?</p>
