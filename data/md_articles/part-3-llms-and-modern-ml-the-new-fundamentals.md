<p>(3rd Part of the 7-Part series on what it takes to be a strong ML engineer in 2026)</p><p>I thought I understood LLMs.</p><p>I mean, I could explain transformers. I knew about attention mechanisms. I had read the “Attention Is All You Need” paper (okay, skimmed it).</p><p>Then I tried to actually build something with LLMs in production.</p><p>Turns out, there’s a big gap between “I can use an API” and “I understand how this actually works and why it fails.”</p><p>Let me share what I’m learning as I try to cross that gap.</p><h3>Tokenization: Where many problems begin (and nobody notices)</h3><p>I’m embarrassed to admit how long I ignored tokenization. It seemed like implementation detail — just the thing that converts text to numbers so the model can process it.</p><p>Wrong. Tokenization is where a lot of subtle problems originate.</p><p><strong>What I’m learning about tokenizers:</strong></p><h3>They fragment your domain-specific language in unpredictable ways:</h3><ul><li>Medical terms get split into meaningless pieces</li><li>Code identifiers break in weird places</li><li>Non-English text gets brutally over-tokenized</li><li>Numbers can be one token or many, depending on formatting</li></ul><p><strong>Real example I tested:</strong></p><ul><li>“cardiovascular” → might be 1 token or 3 tokens depending on the tokenizer</li><li>“2023” → 1 token</li><li>“2,023” → 3 tokens (the comma breaks it)</li><li>“COVID-19” → 2–4 tokens</li></ul><p><strong>Why this matters:</strong></p><h3>Token counts determine your costs:</h3><ul><li>Paying per token, not per word</li><li>That innocent comma just increased your costs by 3x for that number</li><li>Formatting choices affect your monthly bill</li></ul><h3>Token limits are hard walls:</h3><ul><li>8K context means 8K tokens, not 8K words</li><li>Your carefully crafted prompt might get truncated mid-sentence</li><li>That JSON structure you’re asking for? Might not fit</li></ul><h3>Tokenization creates biases:</h3><ul><li>English: ~1 token per word</li><li>Chinese: ~2–3 tokens per character</li><li>Some African languages: even worse</li><li>Same semantic content, very different token costs</li></ul><h3>What I wish I’d known earlier:</h3><ul><li>Always check how your domain-specific text tokenizes</li><li>Test your prompts at different lengths — they might break at token boundaries</li><li>Budget for tokens, not characters</li><li>Different tokenizers behave very differently (GPT vs. Claude vs. Llama)</li></ul><h3>Attention and Context: The fundamental constraints</h3><p>The thing about transformer attention that I didn’t fully appreciate: it’s not just a clever mechanism. It’s a fundamental constraint on what these models can do.</p><p><strong>What “context window” actually means:</strong></p><h3>It’s not just “how much text fits”:</h3><ul><li>It’s how much the model can “pay attention to” simultaneously</li><li>Everything in that window competes for the model’s focus</li><li>More context doesn’t always mean better performance</li><li>The model can “forget” things at the start of a long context</li></ul><h3>Attention is O(n²):</h3><ul><li>Double your context length = 4x the compute</li><li>This is why long context is expensive</li><li>This is why there are hard limits on context size</li><li>This is why clever chunking strategies matter</li></ul><h3>The “lost in the middle” problem:</h3><ul><li>Models pay more attention to the start and end of context</li><li>Information in the middle can get lost</li><li>This breaks naive RAG implementations</li><li>Putting your most important info in the middle is a recipe for failure</li></ul><h3>KV Cache: The thing that determines your serving costs:</h3><p>I didn’t understand KV cache until recently. Now I realize it’s critical for production LLM systems.</p><p><strong>What it is:</strong></p><ul><li>During generation, the model caches key-value pairs from attention</li><li>Reuses them for each new token instead of recomputing</li><li>This is what makes generation feasible at all</li></ul><p><strong>Why it matters:</strong></p><ul><li>KV cache size grows with context length</li><li>It’s often the memory bottleneck in serving</li><li>Longer contexts = more cache = more memory = fewer concurrent requests</li><li>This is why context length and batch size trade off</li></ul><p><strong>What I’m learning:</strong></p><ul><li>Monitor KV cache hit rates in production</li><li>Understand the memory cost of long contexts</li><li>Design prompts with cache efficiency in mind</li><li>Consider whether you really need that full context</li></ul><h3>Fine-tuning vs. LoRA vs. RAG:</h3><h3>Fine-tuning: When you need to change behavior fundamentally</h3><p>Use when:</p><ul><li>You need the model to learn domain-specific patterns</li><li>You have enough quality data (thousands of examples minimum)</li><li>You can afford the compute and storage</li><li>You need consistent behavior across all requests</li></ul><p>Don’t use when:</p><ul><li>Your knowledge changes frequently</li><li>You don’t have enough training data</li><li>You can’t afford full model copies</li><li>You just need to inject some facts</li></ul><h3>LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation): Efficient adaptation</h3><p>Use when:</p><ul><li>You want good amount of fine-tuning benefits but cheaper</li><li>You need multiple adapted versions of the same base model</li><li>You want faster training</li><li>Storage/memory is constrained</li></ul><p>Don’t use when:</p><ul><li>You need to completely change model behavior</li><li>You’re just adding retrievable facts</li><li>Your task is already handled well by the base model</li></ul><h3>RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): Dynamic knowledge</h3><p>Use when:</p><ul><li>Your knowledge updates frequently</li><li>You need to cite sources</li><li>You want explainability</li><li>Your knowledge base is too large for context</li></ul><p>Don’t use when:</p><ul><li>You need the model to internalize patterns, not just retrieve facts</li><li>Your retrieval quality is poor</li><li>Latency is critical</li><li>Your knowledge is small enough to fit in context</li></ul><p><strong>The answer: Often you combine them</strong></p><ul><li>Fine-tune for domain behavior</li><li>Use LoRA for task-specific adaptations</li><li>Add RAG for dynamic, updateable facts</li></ul><h3>RAG: The failure points nobody talks about in tutorials:</h3><p><strong>Chunking destroys semantic coherence:</strong></p><ul><li>Split at 512 tokens? You just broke an explanation in half</li><li>Split at paragraphs? Some paragraphs are 2 words, some are 200</li><li>Split at sentences? Complex documents have sentence dependencies</li><li>No perfect answer. Every strategy has tradeoffs</li></ul><p><strong>Embeddings don’t capture your domain’s notion of relevance:</strong></p><ul><li>General-purpose embeddings optimize for general similarity</li><li>Your domain has specific notions of “relevant”</li><li>“Similar words” ≠ “relevant for this query”</li><li>You might need domain-specific embedding models</li></ul><p><strong>Retrieval finds plausible-sounding but wrong information:</strong></p><ul><li>High semantic similarity ≠ correct answer</li><li>Model retrieves confident-sounding wrong information</li><li>Generates plausible answers from irrelevant context</li></ul><p><strong>Reranking helps but isn’t magic:</strong></p><ul><li>Cross-encoders are slow</li><li>Faster rerankers are less accurate</li><li>Reranking still depends on retrieval quality</li><li>Garbage in, garbage out. Better ranked garbage is still garbage</li></ul><p><strong>Grounding doesn’t actually ground:</strong></p><ul><li>Just because you retrieved relevant docs doesn’t mean the model used them</li><li>Models can ignore retrieved context and hallucinate anyway</li><li>Citation systems can cite irrelevant sources confidently</li><li>Validating that retrieval helped is surprisingly hard</li></ul><p><strong>What I’m learning to do:</strong></p><p><strong>Test retrieval independently:</strong></p><ul><li>Is your retrieval finding the right documents?</li><li>Measure this separately from generation quality</li><li>Track precision and recall of retrieval</li><li>Use retrieval-specific metrics (MRR, nDCG)</li></ul><p><strong>Validate that retrieval actually helps:</strong></p><ul><li>Compare with vs. without retrieval</li><li>Check if the model is actually using retrieved context</li><li>Measure hallucination rates with and without RAG</li><li>Don’t assume retrieval is helping. Measure it. If you can’t measure it, you can’t manage it.</li></ul><p><strong>Monitor in production:</strong></p><ul><li>Track which queries fail to retrieve relevant docs</li><li>Monitor retrieval latency</li><li>Watch for drift in retrieval quality</li><li>Detect when your knowledge base becomes stale</li></ul><h3>Hallucinations: Not bugs, features of the architecture</h3><p>This is the hardest thing for me to accept: hallucinations aren’t bugs to fix with better prompting.</p><p>They’re fundamental to how autoregressive language models work.</p><p><strong>Why models hallucinate:</strong></p><p><strong>They’re trained to always produce plausible text:</strong></p><ul><li>Rewarded for fluent, confident output</li><li>No inherent mechanism for uncertainty</li><li>Will confidently make things up rather than refuse</li></ul><p><strong>They don’t have internal fact databases:</strong></p><ul><li>Knowledge is distributed across parameters</li><li>No clean separation of “known facts” vs “uncertain guesses”</li><li>Can’t check their own knowledge reliably</li><li>Memorization and confabulation look similar</li></ul><p><strong>They are context-limited:</strong></p><ul><li>Can’t always retrieve relevant training information</li><li>Might have seen correct info but can’t access it</li><li>Will fill in gaps with plausible-sounding guesses</li></ul><p><strong>What this means for production:</strong></p><p><strong>You can’t eliminate hallucinations:</strong></p><ul><li>Better prompting helps but isn’t sufficient</li><li>RAG helps but isn’t perfect</li><li>Fine-tuning can reduce but not eliminate</li><li>You need system-level solutions, not just prompt engineering</li></ul><p><strong>Design for hallucinations:</strong></p><ul><li>Add verification steps</li><li>Use retrieval with source citation</li><li>Implement confidence thresholds</li><li>Build human review for critical outputs</li><li>Fail gracefully when uncertain</li></ul><p><strong>Measure hallucination rates:</strong></p><ul><li>Don’t just assume your system is accurate</li><li>Test with known ground truth</li><li>Monitor hallucinations in production</li><li>Track which types of queries cause hallucinations</li></ul><h3>What I have heard from production engineers</h3><p>“Test your system at the token limit. That’s where things break.”</p><p>“RAG quality is 80% retrieval, 20% generation. Fix retrieval first.”</p><p>“Every LLM has different tokenization. Budget separately for each.”</p><p>“Hallucinations will happen. Your system needs to handle them gracefully.”</p><p>“Context length and serving costs trade off directly. Design accordingly.”</p><h3>Tomorrow: Part 4 (Production systems — where models can die)</h3><p>What I’m learning about data pipelines, monitoring, deployment, and why most “model problems” are actually infrastructure problems.</p><p>And as always, if you spotted something I misunderstood, please correct me. I’m here to learn, not to be right.</p>
