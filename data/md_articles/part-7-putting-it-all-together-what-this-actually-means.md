<p>(Final Post of the 7-Part series on what it takes to be a strong ML engineer in 2026)</p><p><strong>Here’s what we’ve learned over these past 6 posts:</strong></p><p>ML engineering in 2026 is vastly more complex than I thought when I started this journey.</p><p>It’s not just about training models. It’s about:</p><ul><li>Understanding data deeply</li><li>Measuring rigorously</li><li>Building reliable systems</li><li>Deploying safely</li><li>Operating at scale</li><li>Working within organizations</li></ul><p>And honestly? I’m still at the beginning of this learning curve.</p><p>But I’m starting to see the path forward more clearly. Let me share what I’m taking away from all this research and learning.</p><h3>Stop Collecting Frameworks. Start Building Systems.</h3><p>This is the biggest mindset shift I’m making.</p><h3>The old approach (what I was doing):</h3><ul><li>Complete tutorial after tutorial</li><li>Add frameworks to my resume</li><li>Build toy projects that work on my laptop</li><li>Move on to the next shiny thing</li></ul><h3>The new approach (what I’m trying to do):</h3><ul><li>Pick one real problem</li><li>Build it end-to-end</li><li>Deploy it to production (even if it’s just for me)</li><li>Watch it break</li><li>Fix it</li><li>Learn from the entire cycle</li></ul><h3>What “end-to-end” actually means:</h3><p>Not just:</p><ul><li>Train a model</li><li>Get good offline metrics</li><li>Call it done</li></ul><p>But:</p><ul><li>Understand the data sources and their failure modes</li><li>Build pipelines that handle real-world messiness</li><li>Implement proper evaluation (offline AND online)</li><li>Deploy with monitoring and observability</li><li>Handle failures gracefully</li><li>Optimize for real constraints (cost, latency)</li><li>Iterate based on production feedback</li></ul><h3>Why this matters:</h3><p>You learn more from one production system than from ten courses.</p><p>Because production teaches you things tutorials can’t:</p><ul><li>How data pipelines break in unexpected ways</li><li>How models degrade silently over time</li><li>How latency requirements force difficult tradeoffs</li><li>How users interact with your system in ways you never imagined</li><li>How organizational constraints shape technical decisions</li></ul><h3>The questions I’m asking myself now:</h3><p>Not “Do I know PyTorch?” but “Can I build a complete ML system?”</p><p>One that:</p><ul><li>Ingests messy production data reliably</li><li>Trains without manual babysitting</li><li>Evaluates performance honestly</li><li>Deploys safely with proper monitoring</li><li>Scales to real usage</li><li>Degrades gracefully when things go wrong</li><li>Provides value that exceeds its costs</li><li>Can be maintained by someone else (or future me)</li></ul><h3>My Learning Plan Going Forward</h3><p>Based on everything I’ve researched and learned, here’s what I’m focusing on:</p><h3>Phase 1: Build one stack deeply</h3><p><strong>Pick PyTorch</strong> (my choice, but JAX is equally valid)</p><p><strong>Go deep:</strong></p><ul><li>Not just .fit() and hope</li><li>But GPU memory management, profiling, optimization</li><li>Understanding what’s actually happening during training</li><li>Being able to debug when things go wrong</li></ul><p><strong>Build something real with it:</strong></p><ul><li>End-to-end pipeline</li><li>Real data with real problems</li><li>Production deployment</li><li>All the unglamorous parts included</li></ul><h3>Phase 2: Master the foundations</h3><h3>Statistics and evaluation:</h3><ul><li>Take a proper statistics course</li><li>Practice A/B testing with real data</li><li>Build evaluation pipelines</li><li>Learn to measure things that matter</li></ul><h3>Data engineering:</h3><ul><li>Learn SQL deeply (embarrassingly important)</li><li>Understand data pipelines</li><li>Practice feature engineering</li><li>Get comfortable with messy data</li></ul><h3>Distributed systems basics:</h3><ul><li>Understand how distributed systems fail</li><li>Learn about queues, retries, idempotency</li><li>Practice building fault-tolerant systems</li><li>Because ML systems are distributed systems</li></ul><h3>Phase 3: Build production experience</h3><h3>Deploy real systems:</h3><ul><li>Start small (personal projects)</li><li>Gradually increase complexity</li><li>Experience the full deployment cycle</li><li>Learn from production failures</li></ul><h3>Focus on the boring parts too:</h3><ul><li>Monitoring and observability</li><li>Deployment automation</li><li>Pipeline reliability</li><li>Documentation</li></ul><h3>Measure everything:</h3><ul><li>Build intuition for what metrics matter</li><li>Practice connecting technical metrics to business value</li><li>Learn to detect problems before they compound</li></ul><h3>Phase 4: Specialize</h3><p>Only after the foundations are solid:</p><ul><li>Pick a specialization (LLMs, computer vision, etc.)</li><li>Go deep on domain-specific challenges</li><li>Build expertise that’s rare and valuable</li></ul><p>But not before. Specialization without foundations is fragile.</p><h3>The Mistakes I’m Making (And Learning From)</h3><p><strong>Being honest about what’s not working:</strong></p><h3>Mistake 1: Starting with optimization too early</h3><ul><li>Tried to optimize before I had something working</li><li>Wasted time on micro-optimizations that didn’t matter</li><li>Should have focused on getting it working first</li><li>Lesson: Premature optimization is real</li></ul><h3>Mistake 2: Underestimating evaluation complexity</h3><ul><li>Thought “I’ll just check if the answers are good”</li><li>Reality: need systematic evaluation or I’m flying blind</li><li>Building proper eval framework retroactively is hard</li><li>Lesson: Build evaluation from day one</li></ul><h3>Mistake 3: Ignoring edge cases initially</h3><ul><li>Focused on happy path. Didn’t think about error handling</li><li>Production immediately hit edge cases I didn’t anticipate</li><li>Lesson: Test failure modes early</li></ul><h3>Mistake 4: Not documenting as I go</h3><ul><li>Told myself “I’ll document it later”</li><li>Later me has no memory of why I made certain decisions</li><li>Now reverse-engineering my own code</li><li>Lesson: Document decisions when you make them</li></ul><h3>Mistake 5: Trying to learn everything at once</h3><ul><li>Started reading about 10 different topics simultaneously</li><li>Got overwhelmed</li><li>Made little progress on any of them</li><li>Lesson: Depth in one area &gt; surface knowledge in many</li></ul><h3>What Success Actually Looks Like</h3><p>I’m redefining what “good ML engineer” means to me.</p><p>Not:</p><ul><li>Can list every framework</li><li>Has certifications in everything</li><li>Knows all the latest papers</li><li>Can talk impressively about transformers</li></ul><p>But:</p><ul><li>Can build complete systems that work in production</li><li>Understands when and why things fail</li><li>Measures rigorously and honestly</li><li>Makes appropriate tradeoffs consciously</li><li>Learns continuously from real systems</li><li>Communicates effectively with stakeholders</li><li>Ships value, not just code</li></ul><p>The shift:</p><p>From “breadth of knowledge” to “depth of capability”</p><p>From “what I know” to “what I can build”</p><p>From “impressive on paper” to “effective in practice”</p><p>What I’m measuring myself on:</p><ul><li>Have I built something that runs in production?</li><li>Does it actually solve a real problem?</li><li>Can I debug it when it breaks?</li><li>Am I measuring its impact honestly?</li><li>Am I learning from each iteration?</li></ul><p>Not:</p><ul><li>How many frameworks do I know?</li><li>How many courses have I completed?</li><li>How impressive does my resume look?</li></ul><h3>What I Need From You</h3><p>If you’ve made it this far through all 7 parts, thank you.</p><p><strong>Here’s how you can help:</strong></p><p><strong>If you’re ahead of me on
